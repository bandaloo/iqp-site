Permanent Magnet History

The 1837 patent for Thomas Davenport's motor does not offer much help for how to produce two semicircular artificial magnets, as all the patent states is that The artificial magnets S T are made of steel and in the usual manner without saying what the usual manner is. The patent also states that galvanic (meaning electromagnetic) magnets, but we wanted to explore what it would take to use create artificial magnets instead. The reader would likely be surprised by how difficult it is to research the subject of the history of developing permanent magnets. The most helpful source that we found, titled "The Early History of The Permanent Magnet" by Edward Neville Da Costa Andrade, makes this observation as well. The paper mentions that little is known about how powerful these centuries-old artificial magnets were, or the precise methods for how these magnets were made. Even though "the history of magnetism has been but little explored" by the author's own admission, the details that are known are surprisingly practical. Most modern information about how to create a magnet requires expensive industrial equipment we (and Thomas Davenport) did not have access to. Ironically, the details from experiments on magnets performed centuries ago by people who did not fully understand electromagnetism were more practical to us than modern-day sources about manufacturing a magnet. However, some of these centuries-old sources contained literal superstition about how magnets functioned. Information such as the fact that garlic and goats' blood could sap magnetic power, were, of course, less practical.

One of the most notable contributors to early permanent artificial magnet research was William Gilbert, who published De Magnete Magneticisque Corporibus et de Magno Magnete Tellure Physiologia Nova in 1600. This book is a rare book that costs more than a luxury car, and we could not find any translations online. Luckily for us, the paper summarizes the three methods that Gilbert outlines for creating permanent magnets. Furthermore, these three methods were the main methods for producing a permanent artificial magnet well into the 19th century (3).

  • 1. Touching a load-stone to a piece of iron very gently, drawing it out from the middle
  • 2. Forging iron in the direction of the Earth's magnetic field
  • 3. While red-hot heat destroys magnetism, leaving a peice of iron to cool in the direction of Earth's magnetic field would acquire magnetic properties.

Option 1 presupposes the existence of another magnet, and is best for creating compass needles. For our motor, we were going to need a much stronger magnet, so the load-stone touching method was not as interesting to us as the other two methods.

We entertained option 2 for a short while, briefly looking at ways to create a backyard forge. However, we deemed that this wouldn't be entirely practical for us.

The third option is interesting, because what Gilbert is observing is the Curie Temperature. Pierre Curie discovered and the laws that govern how magnetism relates to heat in 1895, centuries after Gilbert published his observations about magnetism and red-hot heat in 1600. This also reaffirmed that we were headed in the right direction with our previous attempts where we heated up iron and let it cool in the presence of a magnetic field.

Throughout the 17th century, there were not many new methods being developed. Instead, these methods were refined and improved. However, in the 18th century, there were a few novel methods that were published. One such method was developed by Gowin Knight. (Interestingly, the method was developed in secret, and published after his death.) Instead of a normal bar of iron, he created a "sludge" of iron oxide powder by moving a tub of water and the powder back and forth for a long time. He then hardened the sludge with linseed oil and magnetized the resulting material using existing magnets. Apparently, this resulted in strong magnets (6). While this method was interesting to us since we were having trouble purchasing raw iron (we previously settled on low-carbon steel,) it was clear that this method was particularly involved.

New Plan

Since our previous attempts with a charcoal fire failed, we decided to focus less on being period-accurate with our experiments. Instead of using a charcoal fire, we opted to heat the metal with an acetylene torch to achieve temperatures to surpass the Curie Point. Similarly, we decided to replace the PVC pipe and magnet wire solenoid with a new setup that would also create an approximately uniform field. The solenoid setup we previously had was difficult to use and melted quickly. Instead of relying on the magnetic field of the earth, we purchased two powerful neodymium magnets for the purpose of supplying an external magnetic field. To replace the uniform field created on the inside of a solenoid, the neodymium magnets would act as two plates, which also approximates a uniform magnetic field.
