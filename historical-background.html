Historical Background

Thomas Davenport was an inventor from Williamstown, Vermont. Despite his significant engineering achievement, Davenport comes from humble beginnings. He is the son of Daniel Davenport and Hannah Rice, and was one of their twelve children [5]! He only had three years of formal schooling [1]. At the age of 14, he became an apprentice to a blacksmith [3].

Thomas Davenport became captivated by the potential of electromagnetism in 1831 [4] when he visited Penfield Iron Works in Crown point, NY, which was a hamlet of Ironville [3]. There, he observed an electromagnet that was used for separating iron ore [2]. This magnet, created by Joseph Henry, could lift 750 points of iron. In 1833, he dipped into his savings (and sold his brother's horse) in order to purchase one of Joseph Henry's magnets so he could take it apart and begin studying electromagnetism [3].

In a world where many bright minds were performing similar experiments with electromagnetism, Thomas Davenport's novel invention was discovering that a brush and commutator could be used to reverse the electromagnet's polarity, so the motor could complete a full turn instead of just a half turn. By 1834, he completed his motor and demonstrated it to a professor in Middlebury College in Middlebury, Vermont. In 1835, in Springfield, Massachusetts, he showed his invention to the public

Unfortunately, Thomas Davenport did not find much success with his invention during his lifetime. He died at the young age of 48 [4]. Since he had difficult finding financial backing for his invention, he instead created a journal about electromagnetism and mechanics, whose printing press was powered by his invention [4]. In 1910, decades after his death, Thomas Davenport got some semblance of the respect he deserved. The National Electric Lights Association gave him a level of respect by unveiling a stone tablet in recognition of Thomas Davenport's achievements; there were 500 in attendance, and the ceremony was even complete with a brass band [2]. In volume 56 of Electric World, this was published about his legacy:

"Seen from the industrial standpoint it is significant that if the Davenport patent were enforced today it would embrace every one of the millions of electric motors in use in the United States, whose royalties would constitute an income equal to anything enjoyed by Carnegie or Rockefeller. It would have been a merciful dispensation if the bitter bread of struggle and disaster eaten all the years of his short life by this extraordinary, this prophetic village blacksmith, could have been sweetened with the merest modicum of the vast wealth that his glowing conceptions have helped to create for the benefit of all" [6].
